Tensor Algebra: Covariant and Contravariant Components

1. Definitions

Covariant basis of a vector space of dimension \(n\): \(\mathbf{e_j} \triangleq\) {any linearly independent basis for which in general is \(\mathbf{e_i} \cdot \mathbf{e_j} \neq \delta_{ij}\)}, i.e. not necessarily orthonormal. (D.1)
Contravariant basis \(\mathbf{e^i}\) is the dual basis of \(\mathbf{e_j}\), defined by \(\mathbf{e^i} \cdot \mathbf{e_j} = \delta^i_j\). (D.2)
Proof of duality:
Since \(\mathbf{e^i}\) is dual to \(\mathbf{e_j}\), by definition we have \(\mathbf{e^i} \cdot \mathbf{e_j} = \delta^i_j\). This follows directly from the definition of dual basis in linear algebra.

2. Tensor Components

Any vector \(\mathbf{v}\) can be expressed as: \[ \mathbf{v} = v^j \mathbf{e_j} = v_j \mathbf{e^j} \] where \(v^j\) are the contravariant components and \(v_j\) are the covariant components. (C.1)
Proof:
Using the duality relation \(\mathbf{e^i} \cdot \mathbf{e_j} = \delta^i_j\), we can project \(\mathbf{v}\) onto either basis: \[ \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{e^i} = v^j \mathbf{e_j} \cdot \mathbf{e^i} = v^j \delta^i_j = v^i \] \[ \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{e_i} = v_j \mathbf{e^j} \cdot \mathbf{e_i} = v_j \delta^j_i = v_i \] This shows the relationship between the components.

The metric tensor \(g_{ij}\) lowers indices: \[ v_i = g_{ij} v^j \] while its inverse \(g^{ij}\) raises indices: \[ v^i = g^{ij} v_j \] (D.3)

3. Transformation Rules

Under a change of basis \(\mathbf{e'_i} = A_i^j \mathbf{e_j}\), components transform as:
  • Covariant: \(v'_i = (A^{-1})_i^j v_j\)
  • Contravariant: \(v'^i = A_j^i v^j\)
This opposite transformation behavior gives them the names "co-" and "contra-" variant. (D.4)
