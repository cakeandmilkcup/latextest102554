Introduction to Tensor Analysis

In differential geometry and tensor analysis, we deal with various coordinate systems and basis vectors. Let's explore some fundamental definitions.

Basis Vectors and Components

Covariant basis of a vector space of dimension $n$:

$$\mathbf{e}_j \triangleq \{\text{any linearly independent basis}\}$$

where in general $\mathbf{e}_i \cdot \mathbf{e}_j \neq \delta_{ij}$ (not necessarily orthonormal).

Detailed Explanation of Contravariant Components

For any vector $\mathbf{v}$ in our space, we can express it in terms of basis vectors.

Contravariant components of a vector $\mathbf{v}$ are defined as:

$$v^i \triangleq \{ v^i \mid \mathbf{v} = v^i \mathbf{e}_i \}$$

This uses the Einstein summation convention where repeated indices are summed over.

For example, in 3D space:

$$\mathbf{v} = v^1 \mathbf{e}_1 + v^2 \mathbf{e}_2 + v^3 \mathbf{e}_3$$

The transformation properties of these components under coordinate changes define their "contravariant" nature.

Dual Basis System

Dual (contravariant) basis of a vector space of dimension $n$:

$$\mathbf{e}^i \triangleq \{ \mathbf{e}^i : \mathbf{e}^i \cdot \mathbf{e}_j = \delta^i_j \}$$

Where $\delta^i_j$ is the Kronecker delta:

$$\delta^i_j = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } i = j \\ 0 & \text{if } i \neq j \end{cases}$$

Advanced Tensor Operations

Tensor contraction and index manipulation are essential operations.

Metric Tensor

The metric tensor $g_{ij}$ defines the inner product:

$$g_{ij} = \mathbf{e}_i \cdot \mathbf{e}_j$$

Raising and Lowering Indices

We can convert between covariant and contravariant components:

$$v_i = g_{ij} v^j$$

$$v^i = g^{ij} v_j$$

where $g^{ij}$ is the inverse metric tensor satisfying:

$$g^{ik} g_{kj} = \delta^i_j$$

Christoffel Symbols

For covariant differentiation, we need Christoffel symbols:

$$\Gamma^k_{ij} = \frac{1}{2} g^{km} \left( \frac{\partial g_{jm}}{\partial x^i} + \frac{\partial g_{im}}{\partial x^j} - \frac{\partial g_{ij}}{\partial x^m} \right)$$

Example Calculations

Let's consider a simple 2D example with polar coordinates.

The basis vectors in polar coordinates are:

$$\mathbf{e}_r = \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial r}, \quad \mathbf{e}_\theta = \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial \theta}$$

Where $\mathbf{r} = (r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta)$ in Cartesian coordinates.

Calculating these:

$$\mathbf{e}_r = (\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$$

$$\mathbf{e}_\theta = (-r\sin\theta, r\cos\theta)$$

The metric tensor components:

$$g_{rr} = \mathbf{e}_r \cdot \mathbf{e}_r = 1$$

$$g_{r\theta} = \mathbf{e}_r \cdot \mathbf{e}_\theta = 0$$

$$g_{\theta\theta} = \mathbf{e}_\theta \cdot \mathbf{e}_\theta = r^2$$

So the metric is:

$$g_{ij} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & r^2 \end{pmatrix}$$

And its inverse:

$$g^{ij} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1/r^2 \end{pmatrix}$$
